Your differential diagnosis
Your symptoms don't match a UTI. Here's what they actually match.
Based on your answers, our algorithm identified 2 primary conditions — plus others to rule out. This is your clinical roadmap, not a diagnosis.
Primary conditions to explore
Hormonally mediated vestibulodynia
81%
Testosterone deficiency at the vestibule — often driven by hormonal contraceptives — causes tissue changes that mimic UTI symptoms exactly: burning, urgency, frequency. But it is not an infection. Antibiotics cannot treat it.
Pelvic floor dysfunction
62%
Hypertonic pelvic floor muscles create urinary symptoms, pressure, and pain indistinguishable from a bladder infection. Often co-occurs with vestibulodynia.

Why your bladder and vulva feel the same pain

The vestibule shares nerve pathways with your urethra and bladder. When estrogen drops — often from hormonal birth control — vestibular tissue thins and inflames, creating burning, urgency, and frequency. Same sensations as a UTI. Completely different cause.

🔍 The exam: what to ask for

✓
Vulvar exam with Q-tip (cotton swab) test — locates provoked pain at the vestibule. This is different from a pelvic exam.
✓
No speculum needed. Don't let that stop you from asking.
Request a pelvic floor PT referral

What a vulvar exam looks like: A moistened cotton swab gently tests points around the vestibule for pain response. 2 minutes. Gold standard diagnostic step — and most providers never do it unless you ask.

🧪 Lab work to request

✓
Full hormone panel: estradiol, testosterone, DHEA-S, FSH, progesterone
✓
Urine culture + urinalysis — to formally document negative cultures
Vaginal pH and wet prep microscopy
How to read your estradiol result

Premenopausal range: 30–400 pg/mL. If yours is below 50 while on the pill and you have vestibular symptoms — testosterone suppression is likely driving your pain. A result of 30 is not "normal" for you.
