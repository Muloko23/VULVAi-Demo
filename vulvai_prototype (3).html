Your differential diagnosis
Your symptoms don't match a UTI. Here's what they actually match.
Based on your answers, our algorithm identified 2 primary conditions — plus others to rule out. This is your clinical roadmap, not a diagnosis.
Primary conditions to explore
Hormonally mediated vestibulodynia
81%
Testosterone deficiency at the vestibule — often driven by hormonal contraceptives — causes tissue changes that mimic UTI symptoms exactly: burning, urgency, frequency. But it is not an infection. Antibiotics cannot treat it.
Pelvic floor dysfunction
62%
Hypertonic pelvic floor muscles create urinary symptoms, pressure, and pain indistinguishable from a bladder infection. Often co-occurs with vestibulodynia.
⚡ Also consider when seeing your doctor
Ask your provider to rule out these related conditions:
Interstitial cystitis Vulvar dermatitis Low estrogen atrophy Pudendal neuralgia

Why your bladder and vulva feel the same pain
When testosterone drops, vestibular tissue thins and inflames — creating symptoms identical to a UTI. Same sensations, completely different cause.

Pelvic floor dysfunction — how your muscles affect your bladder
Why antibiotics don't work
The burning is neurogenic, not bacteriological. Cultures are negative or inconsistent. Treating this with antibiotics perpetuates harm and resistance.
What treatment actually looks like
Topical testosterone to the vestibule, discontinuing the hormonal contraceptive driving the suppression, and pelvic floor physical therapy.

Doctor visit toolkit · Part 2
Walk out with a plan.
Know exactly what labs to request — and how to read them yourself.

Lab work to request

✓
Full hormone panel: estradiol, testosterone, DHEA-S, FSH, progesterone
✓
Urine culture + urinalysis — to formally document negative cultures
Vaginal pH and wet prep microscopy
How to read your testosterone result

If yours is below normal while on the pill and you have vestibular symptoms — testosterone suppression is likely driving your pain. A "normal" result on the lab report may not be normal for your body.

Imaging to consider

Pelvic ultrasound to rule out structural causes
Cystoscopy only if interstitial cystitis is suspected
